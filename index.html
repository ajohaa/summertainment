About the Badlands

Nestled in the Black Hills region of western South Dakota, Badlands National Park is a place where dramatic rock formations meet wide-open prairies. Towering spires, colorful layered cliffs, and deep canyons create a landscape unlike anywhere else. Bison, bighorn sheep, and prairie dogs roam the grasslands, offering plenty of wildlife sightings along the way. Visitors can take in sweeping views along the Badlands Loop Road (Highway 240) or set out on trails that begin near the Ben Reifel Visitor Center, the park’s main hub for exploring the North Unit.

History

The history of the Badlands spans millions of years of geological formation, thousands of years of Native American history, and a more recent history as a protected national park.

  • 75 million years ago: A vast inland sea covered the area now known as the Great Plains. Its fossil-rich sediment, known as the Pierre Shale, forms one of the oldest rock layers visible in the Badlands today.
  • 34–28 million years ago: During the Oligocene epoch, the environment transformed from a lush swampland to a drier savanna. This period produced the world's richest deposits of Oligocene mammal fossils, which are a major feature of the Badlands.
  • 500,000 years ago to the present: The modern Badlands landscape began to take shape, carved by rapid erosion from rivers, wind, and rain, as well as deposition. This still happens today.

  • Over 11,000 years ago: The first humans occupied the region, including Paleo-Indians who hunted mammoths. Later, the Arikara, Cheyenne, Kiowa, Pawnee, and Crow used the area as hunting grounds.
  • Around 1700: The Lakota Sioux moved into the northern prairie and displaced other tribes.
  • 18th century: The Lakota began referring to the area as "mako sica," or "bad lands." This name referred to the rugged terrain that was difficult to travel across. French-Canadian trappers adopted a similar term, "les mauvaises terres à traverser" ("bad lands to travel across").
  • 1868: The U.S. government promised the Badlands to the Sioux in the Second Treaty of Fort Laramie, guaranteeing the territory to the tribe "forever".
  • 1889: The treaty was broken by the U.S. government, which confiscated the Badlands area.
  • 1890: A Ghost Dance was held on Stronghold Table. Later that year, the Wounded Knee Massacre occurred roughly 45 miles south of the park on the Pine Ridge Reservation, following a standoff between the U.S. Army and a band of Minneconjou Lakota.
  • 1980: The U.S. Supreme Court awarded compensation to the Sioux for the 1889 treaty violations. However, the Sioux refused the money, preferring the return of the land.

  • 1840s: Fossil finds in the Badlands attracted the first scientific expeditions to the area.
  • Early 1900s: Homesteaders moved into the area, but most failed due to the semi-arid, windswept conditions, which were exacerbated by the Dust Bowl in the 1930s.
  • 1929: Badlands National Monument was authorized by Congress.
  • 1939: The monument was officially established. This occurred around the same time as the development of Mount Rushmore, as part of a larger federal effort to develop tourism in western South Dakota.

  • 1942–1945: During World War II, a 341,726-acre portion of the area on the Pine Ridge Indian Reservation was taken over by the U.S. Army Air Force for a practice gunnery range.
  • 1963–1964: Bison and bighorn sheep were successfully reintroduced to the Badlands.
  • 1968: The U.S. Air Force declared most of its former gunnery range as excess property. The land was returned to the National Park Service, but some unexploded ordnance remains.
  • 1978: The Badlands National Monument was officially redesignated as Badlands National Park.
  • 1990s: The captive-bred black-footed ferret, once thought to be extinct, was reintroduced to the park and is now breeding in the wild.
  • 2003: The swift fox, a species eliminated from the park decades earlier, was successfully reintroduced.
  • 2019: The bison habitat in the park was expanded by 22,000 acres.

Wildlife

Wildlife thrives in every corner of Badlands National Park. From swift foxes darting across the prairie to bighorn sheep navigating steep cliffs, the park is home to creatures both iconic and rare. Each animal adds to the story of this wild and rugged landscape.

Bison

The American bison is the largest land mammal in North America and a symbol of the Great Plains. These massive grazers, weighing up to 2,000 pounds, roam the Badlands in herds, feeding on grasses that help shape the prairie ecosystem. Once nearly driven to extinction, bison now thrive in the park thanks to conservation efforts.

Bighorn Sheep

Bighorn sheep are well adapted to the rugged cliffs and canyons of the Badlands. Their large, curved horns are used for dominance battles between males, while their specialized hooves provide exceptional grip on steep terrain. Once nearly wiped out from the area, they were successfully reintroduced and now thrive in the park’s rocky landscapes.

Prairie Dogs

Prairie dogs are small, social rodents that live in extensive underground colonies called “towns.” These burrowing animals are considered a keystone species because their digging aerates the soil and provides shelter for other wildlife. Visitors can often spot them chirping to warn their neighbors of approaching danger.

Pronghorn

Often mistaken for antelope, pronghorns are North America’s fastest land mammals, capable of reaching speeds up to 55 miles per hour. Their keen eyesight and speed help them evade predators across the open prairie. They migrate seasonally and are well adapted to the wide-open landscapes of the Badlands.

Swift Fox

The swift fox is a small, nocturnal canine named for its incredible speed and agility. Roughly the size of a house cat, it hunts rodents, rabbits, and insects under the cover of darkness. Once extirpated from the region, conservation efforts have helped reestablish this elusive predator in the Badlands ecosystem.

Black-footed Ferret

Among the rarest mammals in North America, black-footed ferrets are nocturnal hunters that rely almost entirely on prairie dogs for food and shelter. Declared extinct in the wild in 1987, they have since been reintroduced to the Badlands as part of an intensive recovery program, making sightings a rare and special treat for visitors.
